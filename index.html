Medicinal Battlefield

Chicago Scientist Fights Antibiotic Resistance

By David Thill

Bacterial infections like pneumonia, tuberculosis and MRSA are becoming resistant to traditional antibiotics faster than new medications can be developed to replace those antibiotics.

But Brian T. Murphy, an associate professor in the University of Illinois at Chicago’s College of Pharmacy, thinks he can help. Murphy heads a research team that examines ocean-based bacterial microbes, part of a larger effort to speed up the somewhat trial-and-error process of antibiotic development.

Drug-resistant infections result in 8 million additional days spent in American hospitals each year, Murphy said in a July talk at the Radler restaurant in Logan Square. He added that those infections cost the American health care system between $21 billion and $34 billion annually.

“And I can tell you for a fact this is going to get worse,” he added, noting a decrease since the 1990s in pharmaceutical manufacturer-backed research and antibiotic development.

Murphy’s lab focuses on the “front end” of what can be a decades-long process of developing the medications doctors prescribe to fight infections. His work includes the collection and identification of potential antibacterial agents from oceans and lakes.

To make that process more efficient, Murphy’s lab employs a technique called mass spectrometry to reveal the unique components – the ones needed to develop antibiotics – possessed by the microbes he collects.

Through this process, he aims to narrow the search for antibacterial agents. For example, he said, rather than developing a library of 3,000 microbial samples from which to choose for further research, he could develop a library of only 300 samples with greater diversity, thus allowing for faster antibiotic development.

Murphy noted that his mass spectrometry research, now in its second year, has yet to be peer-reviewed. At press time, he said he hoped to begin that peer-review process in August. But he said other researchers in the field of antibiotic development have expressed support for the work.

Sponsored by the Chicago Council on Science and Technology, Murphy’s talk brought together about 60 community members, from scientific researchers to mental health providers to students – many with a beer in hand – to hear Murphy speak.

Andrea Poet, the Council’s director of programs and public relations, said the Council hopes talks such as Murphy’s, presented in bars like the Radler, draw a more general audience than, for example, a talk in a university lecture hall.

She cited low scientific literacy rates among U.S. adults as a primary reason for these public events. A Michigan State University researcher in 2007 pegged the national adult scientific literacy rate at about 28 percent.

Poet admitted that certain talks, including some the Council presented on Alzheimer’s, don’t necessarily draw such a wide audience. But she said others – like a recurring “Physics of Baseball” talk – have proved popular among the general public (especially baseball fans).
